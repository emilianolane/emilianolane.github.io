Transcendentalism

by Emiliano Lane | 5/19/2023

What is Transcendentalism?

Transcendentalism is an ideology that formed in the early 19th century that holds several key principles:

  • Humans are inherently good. However, humans can be corrupted by society, large institutions, etc. Because of this, people are at their best when they are independent.
  • Experience and insight are more important than logic. Transcendentalists prioritized creativity and imagination over logic as well.
  • Nature is amazing and beautiful, and it should be respected by humans, especially because we rely on nature to survive. Nature is created to be inherently good, just as humans are.

History of Transcendentalism

Transcendentalism emerged in the 19th and originated in the New England region in the United States. Ralph Waldo Emerson's essay "Nature" in 1836 laid the foundation for transcendentalist thought, opposing existing rationalism. The Transcendental Club was created in 1940, and it served as a place for influential Transcendentalists such as Emerson, Henry David Thoreau, and Margaret Fuller to exchange ideas. Thoreau's book "Walden; or, Life in the Woods" exemplified transcendentalist ideals, advocating for simplicity and a deep connection with nature. While the formal movement waned in the late 19th century, transcendentalism left a lasting impact on American thought and literature, inspiring subsequent generations of writers and thinkers.

Transcendentalists actively engaged in social and political reform, particularly during the American Civil War. Many transcendentalists, including Emerson and Thoreau, actively supported the abolitionist movement, advocating for the end of slavery and promoting human rights. Their involvement demonstrated the movement's commitment to social justice and equality. Although the formal transcendentalist movement declined, its emphasis on individualism, self-reliance, and the pursuit of truth resonated with those seeking a deeper connection with themselves and the natural world. Transcendentalist works continue to be influential, shaping American intellectual and cultural history.
